Investigation Guide
Best practices for conducting workplace violence investigations
Quality Investigation Checklist
Complete all steps within 30 days of the incident. 48-hour preliminary must be done first.
Within 48 hours: Contact the injured employee to acknowledge the incident, confirm they are safe, and communicate next steps.
Review all documentation: PWHCC incident report, employee statement, security report, patient/visitor records.
Conduct scene review: Visit the location, identify environmental hazards, photograph if appropriate.
Interview witnesses: Use open-ended questions. Document statements factually without leading language.
Employee interview: Use a trauma-informed approach (see Trauma-Informed section). Focus on facts, not blame.
Identify contributing factors: Use the taxonomy in Section 9. Look for systemic causes, not just individual behaviour.
Develop corrective actions: Each action should be SMART — specific, measurable, assigned to an owner with a due date.
Submit to Web IIT: Enter all findings within 30 days. Notify your Client Partner upon completion.
Key Principles
Focus on systems, not individuals. Most incidents have multiple contributing factors. Look for gaps in processes, environment, training, and communication — not just individual errors.
Maintain confidentiality. Investigation details are shared only on a need-to-know basis. Do not discuss specifics with non-involved staff.
Be factual, not interpretive. Document what was observed and reported — not your conclusions about why someone behaved a certain way.
Timelines matter. The 48-hour contact and 30-day submission deadlines are regulatory requirements under the Workers Compensation Act.
Trauma-Informed Interview Approach
Guidance for meeting with the injured employee post-incident
Before the Meeting

• Choose a private, comfortable location — not your office if power dynamics could feel uncomfortable.

• Ask the employee what they need before you begin: a glass of water, a support person, a specific time.

• Let them know the purpose of the meeting is to understand what happened to improve safety — not to evaluate their performance.

• Remind them that participation is voluntary and they can take breaks at any time.

During the Interview

• Start by acknowledging the impact: "I want to start by saying I'm sorry this happened to you."

• Use open-ended questions: "Can you walk me through what you remember?" — not "Why didn't you call security sooner?"

• Follow the employee's pace. Do not rush or fill silences immediately.

• Validate their emotional response: "That sounds like a very frightening situation."

• If they become distressed, pause: "We can take a break — this is not urgent."

• Focus on facts and their experience — avoid asking them to speculate about the perpetrator's intentions.

• Do not take notes visibly throughout — it can feel interrogative. Summarize in writing after.

After the Meeting

• Thank them for their time and courage in sharing what happened.

• Confirm next steps: what you will do with the information and when you'll follow up.

• Reiterate EAP and other support resources.

• Schedule a follow-up check-in (1 week, 1 month) to see how they are doing.

• Document only factual information — do not include emotional commentary in the formal record.
